From $30 to $3: What U.S. Gas Storage Knew That the Market Didn't

By Sristi Shreya  |  June 2026  |  Natural Gas Market Analysis

On January 23, 2026, natural gas hit $30.72 per MMBtu at Henry Hub. If you don't know what that means, imagine your gas bill tripling overnight. That's the kind of number that makes energy traders sweat through their shirts.

And look, the fear made sense. The Iran conflict was escalating. Europe and Asia were nervous, the kind of nervous where you stop sleeping and start making phone calls. They rushed to lock in U.S. LNG contracts as a backup. If Middle Eastern flows got disrupted, they needed an alternative. Yesterday.

So traders did what traders do. They followed the headline. Less gas leaving for exports means less staying home. Less staying home means storage drains. Storage drains means prices explode. Simple math. Rational fear. Completely wrong conclusion.

Chart 1 — Henry Hub Spot Price, Jan 2025 to Jun 2026

Here's what the market missed.

U.S. natural gas production had just quietly hit a record 109 billion cubic feet per day. Not because of luck. The Trump administration had spent a year cutting regulatory red tape, fast-tracking permits, and telling producers to drill like the economy depended on it, because they believed it did. The supply machine was already running at full speed before the panic even started.

So when Europe and Asia came rushing for U.S. LNG, the system absorbed it. Exports went up. And there was still enough gas left over to keep filling storage. The feared drain never came.

Winter ended. Demand dropped. And storage just kept building.

By June 2026, U.S. storage sat at 2,686 Bcf, a surplus of 276 Bcf above the 5-year average. To put that in perspective, the past five years have been a rollercoaster. COVID demand collapse. The 2021 Texas freeze. The post-Ukraine energy shock. Through all of it, storage swung wildly. A 276 Bcf surplus in this environment isn't just a number. It's a signal.

Chart 2 — U.S. Natural Gas Storage vs. 5-Year Average

Prices, predictably, collapsed. $30.72 in January. $3.05 by June. A 90% crash in four months.

What does this mean for winter 2026-27? Honestly, it's the best setup the market has had in years. High inventory gives plants breathing room to go offline for maintenance without triggering a supply crisis. It keeps a lid on price spikes even if demand surprises to the upside. And it gives utilities, industrials, and LNG exporters the confidence to plan without one eye on the weather forecast at all times.

The market panicked. The storage data didn't.

It rarely does.
